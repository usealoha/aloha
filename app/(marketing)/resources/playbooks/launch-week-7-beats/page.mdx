Most launches lose more reach to *bad scheduling* than to bad
copy. The piece is good; the timing is off; the third post never
shipped because Wednesday got busy. The seven-beat plan below is
how creators with no team consistently out-perform creators with
five. It's mechanical, opinionated, and works in any
content-production reality I've seen.

This is what happens at six weeks before launch, on launch day,
and over the seven days after. Each beat names what to publish,
where, and roughly when.

## The principle

Audiences don't show up because they're paying close attention
the day you launch. They show up because the launch *catches them
on a different day*. The seven-beat plan multiplies the surface
area of "different day."

Read [the seventh telling](/resources/field-notes/seventh-telling)
for the longer essay; this playbook is the operational version.

## Beat 1 — Six weeks out: The seed

A single post, one channel, that names what you're working on
without any of the sales energy. The point is to plant the
expectation, not to sell anything. Examples:

- A field-note style post: "Spent the last few months thinking
  about X. About to start sharing what I learned."
- A short reply in a community space: "Working on Y. Anyone else
  wrestling with this?"

What to write it in: the [Composer voice model](/composer) so it
genuinely sounds like the rest of your work, not a launch
announcement.

## Beat 2 — Three weeks out: The teaser

A short, specific tease that hints at the form. Show the *shape*
of the thing, not the thing itself. A photo of the work in
progress. A pull quote from the introduction. A note about why
you're making this — not what it does.

Cross-post to two channels. The Composer should rewrite for
each — long for LinkedIn, sharp for X. Don't manually re-type;
that's where most launches lose 90 minutes.

## Beat 3 — One week out: The "soon" post

The first post that's *explicitly* about the launch. Time-stamped:
"a week from today." A clear what-and-when, plus a low-friction
way to be notified. Email signup, follow notification, a calendar
invite.

This is the post most creators skip and most successful launches
lean on. It catches the people who like you but don't check your
feed daily.

## Beat 4 — Launch day: The drop

The actual launch post. Across every channel that matters for
this work. Two important rules:

1. **Schedule it the day before.** Don't post manually on launch
   day; the launch day is for replying to early reactions, not
   wrestling with the scheduler.
2. **Use the Composer's per-channel rewrite** rather than copy-
   pasting. LinkedIn wants long; X wants tight; Threads wants
   conversational. Same idea, three native versions.

Pin the post to your profile if the platform allows it.

## Beat 5 — Day +1: Social proof

Find one early reaction that captures something true and share
it. Not "look at this nice review of my thing" — instead share
the thought the reaction sparked. The work is the focus; the
proof is the side door.

If no organic reaction has landed yet, share a behind-the-scenes
detail instead. The construction of the work is content too.

<blockquote>
A launch is not a single post — it's a story you tell over six
weeks. Most creators tell three chapters and wonder why nobody
remembers chapter one.
</blockquote>

## Beat 6 — Day +3: The objection

Whatever question keeps coming up in your DMs and comments —
write the answer publicly. "A few people asked X. Here's the
short version." This catches the audience that almost engaged
but had a quiet doubt that stopped them.

Keep it short. The objection post isn't the place for nuance;
it's the place for clarity. If the answer is genuinely complex,
write a longer field note for [Resources](/resources/field-notes)
and link it.

## Beat 7 — Day +7: The "for the late arrivals" post

A retrospective, gentle re-surfacing for the people who missed
the launch. "Last week I shipped X. Here's where to start if
you're just hearing about it." This is the post that catches the
people who come back from holiday and scroll your last week.

Treat this post as the *true close* of the launch — not the
launch day itself.

## What the calendar looks like

Aloha's [Calendar](/calendar) accepts a campaign template you can
clone in two clicks: "Launch week — 7 beats." It pre-populates the
seven posts on the right days and lets you fill in the bodies in
order. The matrix wires the cross-channel rewrites automatically
so you only edit once.

If you don't use Aloha, build the same seven beats in whatever
your scheduler is. Naming them helps. Treat each beat as a
non-negotiable; if you can't ship a beat, move it, but don't
silently drop it.

## What this playbook isn't

This isn't a growth-hacking framework. It won't 10x your launch.
It will, reliably, get more of the audience you already have to
notice the work — which is almost always where launches actually
fail.

You can run this for a tiny launch (a single newsletter issue) or
a big one (a product, a book, a major campaign). Adjust the
content per beat; keep the cadence.

Ship the work, then tell the story it deserves. Seven times,
across six weeks. Not because the algorithm needs it — because
the audience can't find you in one pass.
