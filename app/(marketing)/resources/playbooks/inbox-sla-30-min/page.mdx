There's a tax small businesses don't talk about. It's the
"someone-needs-to-be-on" tax — the silent expectation that one
person on the team is always near a phone in case a customer
DMs at 9pm.

This tax is bad for the team and bad for the customer. Bad for
the team because nobody actually committed to being on. Bad for
the customer because the response time is *unreliable* — sometimes
fast, sometimes a day, sometimes never.

This playbook is the four rules we wrote at Aloha when we
started Aloha-the-business and had this exact problem. It turns
"always on" into "reliably responsive in 30 minutes during
weekday business hours." Most small teams can run it with three
or four people.

## The four rules

### 1. Define the SLA *out loud*

Pick a number. Ours is **30 minutes during weekday business
hours** (IST morning + late afternoon). Communicate it publicly —
on the link-in-bio, on the contact page, in the auto-reply. The
SLA isn't a promise to be on, it's a promise to *say* when you're
on.

The auto-reply matters as much as the SLA itself. We use the
[Inbox](/inbox) to set one that says: "We see your message. A
human will reply within 30 minutes between 10am-1pm and 5-7pm IST.
Outside those hours, by 10am the next business day."

Customers who get this auto-reply almost never message again to
ask if you're there. The SLA does the calming.

### 2. One person on at a time, never overlapping

The "everyone is on Slack on weekends" pattern fails because
nobody knows whose job it is. The fix: a single named person on
during the SLA window, with explicit hand-offs.

A team of three covers two two-hour windows a day, three days a
week each. That's the entire structure. The Inbox surfaces unread
threads to the on-person; the off-people don't see notifications.

### 3. Triage, don't reply

The 30-minute SLA is for *triage*, not full reply. The on-person
opens the message and decides:

- **Quick answer** — reply now (under five minutes).
- **Real answer needed** — acknowledge now ("Thanks — I'll get
  you a real answer by tomorrow morning"), put a real reply on
  tomorrow's calendar.
- **Wrong inbox** — reroute to the right person, tag them, close
  the thread on this side.

The Inbox triage view sorts by message shape — questions, praise,
needs-review — so this decision takes about ten seconds per
thread.

<blockquote>
You don't need to be always on to feel responsive. You need to
be reliably present in announced windows.
</blockquote>

### 4. Auto-handle the easy ones

Praise, emoji reactions, "thanks for following," people simply
saying hi — these don't need the on-person's time. The Inbox can
auto-reply with a warm one-line response on a per-thread basis;
the on-person just confirms with one tap.

We measured this at Aloha: about 60% of weekday inbox volume
falls into the auto-handle category. Removing it from the
on-person's queue is the difference between a manageable shift
and a frantic one.

## What the schedule looks like

For a three-person team:

| Person | Mon-Wed | Thu-Fri |
| --- | --- | --- |
| A | 9-11am, 4-6pm | rest |
| B | rest | 9-11am, 4-6pm |
| C | covers both halves | covers both halves |

C is the rotating gap-filler — one of the three rotates into the
"covers" seat each week so nobody burns out on the edges. Holidays
and sick days swap into the covers seat first.

## What this playbook isn't

This isn't customer-care at scale. If you have hundreds of DMs a
day, you need a real CS tool ([Sprout Social](/compare/sprout-social)
is good for that). This is for the team that has 30-50 weekday
DMs and wants to feel responsive without anyone feeling
permanently on.

It's also not 24-hour coverage. Weekend and after-hours messages
get the auto-reply explicitly setting expectations. Customers
respect this when you're transparent.

## The compounding effect

Six months in, our team has not lost a single weekend, and our
customer satisfaction on response time is the highest it's ever
been. The two are causally related. Reliable presence beats
performative availability every time.

If you set this up and the SLA turns out to be wrong for your
volume — you're getting more or less than expected — adjust the
windows, not the rule. The shape of the playbook holds.
