Good tools have a default state of *you're done*. That's the state
where the tool has done its job, and the reasonable response is to
close the tab and go do something else. Most software is designed
to prevent this from ever happening.

The product manager's job, in a lot of companies, is to design
against "you're done." Every screen becomes a gateway to a next
screen. Every notification earns a badge. Every chart ends with a
call to action. Time-on-page goes up and so does the churn number
that nobody looks at.

We wrote **"the default state of a good tool is you're done"** on
the wall of the Bengaluru studio early in 2025. Then we spent a
quarter finding five places in Aloha where we had broken the rule
by accident. This is a note about those five places — not to beat
ourselves up, but because they're the kinds of mistakes everyone
makes if they don't watch for them.

## 1. The notification badge that didn't need to exist

Sometime in Q3 of the private beta, we shipped a notification that
told you when your scheduled post went live. It had a red badge.
Users loved it — briefly. We watched the session data and saw
something we didn't love: the app was teaching people to check it
after every post shipped, even though nothing about that check
affected the outcome. Post shipped. You can close the tab.

We replaced it with a once-a-day digest. Session length dropped.
Customer satisfaction went up. These were correlated, not in
conflict.

## 2. The daily-streak panel

For about two weeks, the home dashboard carried a "days in a row
you've posted" counter. It was meant to motivate creators who
wanted a cadence. What it actually did was create small panic on
day 11 when someone missed a day — *because they chose to skip* —
and felt their streak reset as a judgement.

We killed it. We kept the underlying cadence data because it's
useful; we stopped turning it into a badge.

## 3. "Your week in review" that wasn't earned

The Monday morning email reviewed "what you shipped last week." It
had strong open rates. It also showed up when you'd shipped one
thing, when you'd shipped nothing, when you were on holiday. The
strong open rates were dread, not interest.

Monday morning review became an opt-in for accounts with >5 posts
shipped in the week. The rest get silence on Monday, which is
often what they need.

<blockquote>
The easiest way to spot a bad default is to ask: what does it feel
like on a bad day?
</blockquote>

## 4. The "similar creators to you" tray

We added a tray on the analytics view showing creators in your
bucket with better engagement. It was intended to spark ideas. It
mostly sparked comparison anxiety. "Why is Theo getting 10× my
reach" is not the question anyone wants to answer at 11pm on a
Tuesday.

Removed. Ideas still come from the [Composer templates
page](/resources/templates) and from the [creator spotlights](/customers/spotlight),
which are curated, not algorithmic.

## 5. The celebratory confetti

Confetti on a successful schedule. This was the one I fought
hardest for — I'd seen it in a dozen other apps — and it was the
most obviously wrong once we looked at it. Creators are
scheduling *hundreds of things a quarter*. A small dopamine hit
on every one trains you to find the feeling of posting more
meaningful than the post itself.

Replaced with a small acknowledgement chip that fades after 3
seconds. The post is saved. Move on.

## The test we use now

Before any notification, badge, review email or engagement prompt
goes out, we ask four questions:

1. Does it make the user's actual work easier, or just make the
   tool feel more alive?
2. Does it still make sense if the user is on a break or a bad day?
3. If we remove it, does the user notice?
4. What cadence would a careful human editor choose for this
   message? (Not daily. Usually weekly, sometimes monthly.)

Most things that pass (1) fail on (2) and (3). That's the place
to cut.

The default state of Aloha, when nothing is broken and nothing is
urgent, should be absence. You finish your work. You close the
tab. We did our job when you forgot you were paying us for a
while.
