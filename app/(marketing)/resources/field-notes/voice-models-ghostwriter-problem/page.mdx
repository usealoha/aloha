When we started building **Muse** — the voice model inside
[the Composer](/composer) — we asked our beta testers for their
archives. We wanted as much data as possible; the instinct was
that more training material would mean better outputs. The first
generation of Muse trained on the last twelve hundred posts a
user had published.

The outputs were flat.

They were technically *correct* — the grammar was right, the
length was right, the hashtags were in the right places — and
yet none of them sounded like the creators they were modelling.
They sounded like the *aggregate* of those creators: a smoothed
average of twelve hundred moments, with all the texture rounded
off.

This is the ghostwriter problem. When a human ghostwriter starts
working with a client, they don't read the client's whole
archive. They read the three or four pieces the client is
proudest of — the ones that sound most like the voice the client
*wants* to be known for — and they learn from those. The rest of
the archive is noise.

## Why the last 1,200 are mostly noise

Most creators publish a lot of mediocre work. This isn't an
insult; it's a feature of the medium. You learn by shipping. A
creator's last twelve hundred posts contain:

- The twelve or twenty that really sounded like them.
- A few hundred that were competent but interchangeable.
- Hundreds more that were written in a hurry, in a state of
  distraction, or in response to someone else's viral moment.
- Maybe a hundred or so that the creator would actively remove
  if they could — phrases they grew out of, patterns they tried
  once and hated.

Training on all 1,200 produces a model that knows the creator's
grammar and none of their *feel*. The creator themselves can
tell. When they read a model-drafted rewrite, the first reaction
is "this is close but it's not *me*." They're right.

## The 12-post rule

We rewrote the training pipeline in 2025. The new version asks
the creator to do one short task before the model starts: pick
the twelve posts that sound most like you. Not the ones that got
the most engagement — the ones that *sound most like you*. Those
are often different posts.

The resulting model is dramatically better. Voice-match scores
jumped from about 78% to 94% on our internal benchmark. More
importantly, the creators who use the Composer stopped saying
"this is close but" and started saying "I can't tell which
drafts I wrote and which Aloha did."

<blockquote>
A good voice model is an editor's intuition, captured. Editors
don't read your archive — they read the work you're proud of.
</blockquote>

## What "sounds like me" actually means

When we ask creators to pick their twelve voice-representative
posts, they almost always notice a pattern they hadn't fully
articulated. Someone who thought they wrote "tight, punchy
single-tweet takes" realises their best work is actually
medium-length threads with a specific hook pattern. Someone who
thought of themselves as a long-form writer realises their twelve
best posts are all under 500 words.

The model captures this pattern in a way that a spec written in
English couldn't. You don't need to know that you lean on
em-dashes and never use semicolons. You just need to point at
posts where both of those things are true. The model infers the
rest.

## The ghostwriter problem persists

I want to be careful here. The voice model isn't a replacement
for a ghostwriter, for two reasons. One, a ghostwriter brings
*judgement* — they know which ideas are worth writing about at
all. The model takes the idea as given and renders it in your
voice; it doesn't decide what to write about. Two, a ghostwriter
evolves with you. They notice when your voice shifts and lean
into the shift. The model re-trains on demand, but the shift has
to be noticed by a human first.

What the voice model *is* good for is the 80% of posting where
the idea is clear and the rendering is the part that takes 45
minutes. That 45 minutes, across eight channels, across a year,
is what wrecks most creators' relationship with their own work.
The model gives the time back.

## What this means for the way you train it

If you're setting up a voice model — ours, or anyone else's — I
have one piece of advice. Don't dump your archive. Pick twelve.
Pick them slowly. Read each one once and ask: *does this sound
like the version of me I want people to read?* If the answer is
no, skip it, even if it did well.

Twelve is a small number. That's the point.
