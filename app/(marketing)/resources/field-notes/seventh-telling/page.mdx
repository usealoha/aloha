Last quarter I launched a newsletter issue. I posted about it on X
twice, on LinkedIn once, and in a Slack community. Roughly 14,000
people saw those posts. The newsletter got 48 new subscribers.

Two weeks later I posted about the same issue again — not a
recycle, a reframe. "Here's what happened because of this piece."
Another 3,200 people saw it. 112 new subscribers.

Two weeks after that, a member emailed to ask if I could link the
piece they'd heard me mention on a podcast. The podcast episode
had gone out four days after the original launch. That email
triggered a seventh re-surfacing — a quote post, with the member's
permission — which drove another 86 subs.

By my count, I had told people about the piece **seven distinct
times** across about six weeks. The final number of subscribers
was 412. The first three tellings had produced 48. The next four
produced 364.

This is not a story about social algorithms. It's a story about
patience, and about the ratio nobody tells you.

## The ratio

Making the thing is 20% of the job. Caring enough to tell people
about it — seven times, ten times, on whatever channel they
happen to be on — is the other 80%. Almost nobody writes about
this ratio, because it's embarrassing. Most of us want the work
to sell itself; it rarely does.

The first telling reaches the people who were already paying
attention. That's roughly 10% of your eventual audience for the
piece. They respond quickly, which is confusing — they make you
think you're done. You're not.

The second through fourth tellings reach the people who were
almost paying attention. They saw the first telling and kept
scrolling. They see the second or third and remember the first.
Their mental model of your work gets one degree more specific
every time. You pick up another 20%.

The fifth through seventh tellings reach the people who weren't
paying attention the first time. Their friend shared it. They
saw it referenced in a podcast. They saw a quote that wasn't the
original. *These are where most of your eventual audience comes
from.*

<blockquote>
The seventh telling is when the audience you weren't sure existed
finally answers.
</blockquote>

## Why most creators stop at three

We stop at three because telling a fourth time feels like
self-promotion. We've internalised a norm that equates repetition
with desperation. This norm is, I think, almost entirely wrong.
Repetition looks like desperation when the thing being repeated
isn't worth repeating. When it is, repetition looks like care.

The other reason we stop is operational. The third telling is
usually a different format — a quote post, a reply, a mention —
and by the third we've exhausted our obvious hooks. Without a
tool that helps us keep going, we peter out.

The Composer, for whatever it's worth, was built with this exact
problem in mind. Rewriting the same piece for four channels on
launch day is one thing. [The seven-beat launch template](/resources/templates)
is what happens when you want to do it properly.

## A rule you can steal

If you ship something you're proud of, commit in advance to
seven tellings. Write them down. Space them across six weeks.
Vary the format — first-person, quote, reply, community-member-
shared, podcast-reference, "here's what I learned after posting
this", "did anyone else notice this."

Put them in your calendar. Treat the calendar as a promise to
your past self. The version of you that shipped the thing wanted
the world to know. The version of you six weeks later has
already moved on. The calendar is how those two selves
negotiate.

## The quiet version of this

This rule can be practised with restraint. Seven tellings in six
weeks is one per week; it's not a daily push. You don't need to
be loud. You need to be *present*. The audience you're trying to
reach is distracted — you're competing with everybody else's
content for thirty seconds of attention. Being present seven
times over six weeks is a small ask. It's also the minimum.

Ship the work, then tell the story it deserves. Seven times. Not
because the algorithm needs it, but because the audience can't
find you in one pass.
